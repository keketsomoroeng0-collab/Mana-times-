"I have lost everything" — Mthokozisi Ndaba faces eviction and divorce woes

Ntuzuma, Durban — Mthokozisi Ndaba, a 34-year-old former logistics coordinator, says his life has crumbled in ways he never imagined possible. On Monday, he was released on R500 bail from the Ntuzuma magistrate's court after spending a night behind bars following a physical altercation with his cousin Vukile Jaca, who opened a case of assault against him.

But the court appearance is only the latest chapter in a devastating downward spiral that has seen Ndaba lose his job, face imminent eviction from his rented flat, and watch his seven-year marriage fall apart — all in the span of four months.

"I wake up some mornings and I don't even recognize myself," Ndaba said in an exclusive interview with Campus Vibe. "Last year this time, I had a wife, a home, a steady income. Now I have nothing. Absolutely nothing."

The incident that landed Ndaba in police custody began as a dispute over borrowed money. According to court documents, Ndaba had lent his cousin Vukile Jaca R3,500 in December to help cover Jaca's daughter's school fees. When Ndaba requested repayment last week — desperate after being served an eviction notice — an argument escalated into a physical confrontation outside Jaca's home in Ntuzuma.

Neighbors who witnessed the altercation described seeing the two men shouting at each other before Ndaba allegedly pushed Jaca against a wall. Jaca sustained minor bruising to his shoulder and arm and opened a case at the Ntuzuma SAPS the following day.

"I was frustrated, I won't deny that," Ndaba said, his voice heavy with regret. "But Vukile knows me. He knows I wouldn't hurt him intentionally. We grew up together, went to the same crèche. Now he's treating me like a criminal."

Jaca declined to comment for this story but confirmed through a family spokesperson that he has no plans to withdraw the charges. "A line was crossed," the spokesperson said. "Blood doesn't give anyone the right to put their hands on you."

For Ndaba, the assault charge is merely the public face of a private tragedy that began in November 2025, when his employer of eight years — a Durban-based freight company — announced mass retrenchments amid economic downturn. Ndaba was one of 47 workers let go without severance pay after the company declared provisional liquidation.

"I gave that company everything," he said, staring at the floor of the small, sparsely furnished flat he now fears he'll soon be forced to leave. "I worked weekends, I covered shifts when no one else would. And then one day, the doors were locked and security wouldn't let us in."

By January, the strain of unemployment had fractured his marriage. His wife, Nomonde Ndaba, moved out with their two children, citing "irreconcilable differences" and "financial pressure." Divorce papers were filed last month.

"She says she still loves me, but she can't live like this," Ndaba said, his eyes welling up. "I understand. I really do. A man is supposed to provide. When I couldn't provide anymore, what was left for her to hold onto?"

Nomonde could not be reached for comment, but a close friend of hers told Campus Vibe that "the relationship had problems long before the retrenchment. The money issues just brought everything to the surface."

Compounding his legal and marital troubles, Ndaba now faces eviction from the flat in Inanda where he has lived since 2021. Landlord Thokozani Mkhize confirmed that Ndaba is three months behind on rent, owing R12,000 in arrears.

"I've been patient because I know his situation," Mkhize said in a phone interview. "But I have my own bills, my own family to support. I've given him until the end of the month to vacate or come up with something. I don't want to take him to court, but I will if I have to."

Ndaba says he has no family members willing or able to take him in. His parents, both retired, live in a cramped two-room house in KwaMashu that already houses six other relatives. "I can't go back there. There's literally no space, and my father is sick — I don't want to add to his stress."

Despite the cascading crises, Ndaba says he is trying to hold on to hope. He has applied for more than 60 jobs since November, he says, with only two interviews — neither of which led to an offer. He spends his days at a local internet cafe, printing CVs and walking door-to-door at warehouses and factories across Durban's industrial areas.

"Someone has to say yes eventually, right?" he said, managing a faint smile. "I'm not a lazy person. I want to work. I want to be able to look my children in the eye again and tell them Daddy is back on his feet."

"I'm asking anyone who can help — even if it's just a temporary position, a week's work, anything. I'm not too proud to scrub floors or load trucks. I just need a chance."

His children — a daughter, 6, and son, 4 — now live with their mother in Pietermaritzburg, nearly 80 kilometers away. Ndaba says he hasn't seen them in more than a month.

"My daughter asked me on the phone last week why I don't come to her school play anymore," he said, his voice breaking. "I didn't know what to tell her. I didn't have the money for transport. How do you explain that to a six-year-old?"

News of Ndaba's situation has begun to circulate in his community, prompting a small but growing network of support. A neighbor, Gugu Mthembu, has organized a collection among residents in his apartment block, gathering groceries and a few hundred rand to help him get by.

"Mthokozisi is a good man who has fallen on hard times," Mthembu said. "It could happen to any of us. We're not rich people here, but we give what we can because that's what community is about."

The local branch of the South African Depression and Anxiety Group (SADAG) has also reached out, offering free counseling sessions. Ndaba said he attended his first session this week.

"I didn't think I needed help with my head," he admitted. "I thought I just needed a job and money. But sitting there, talking to someone who actually listened... I realized I've been carrying a lot of anger and sadness that I didn't know how to deal with."

Ndaba is due back in court on April 15 for a formal bail hearing. His legal aid attorney has advised him that the assault charge could result in a fine or suspended sentence if he pleads guilty — but a criminal record would make finding employment even more difficult.

Meanwhile, the eviction deadline looms. With no job prospects in sight and dwindling savings, Ndaba says he has started packing his belongings into cardboard boxes, preparing for the worst while praying for a miracle.

"I'm not giving up," he said firmly, straightening in his chair. "I can't. I have children who need to see that their father is a fighter, not someone who folds when life gets hard. I may have lost my job, my marriage, soon my home. But I haven't lost myself."

He paused, looking out the window at the dusty street below.

"At least, I hope I haven't."

Building a Responsible AI Future: Strengthening Governance & Innovation in Lesotho

Maseru, Lesotho — In a modest conference room at the Lesotho National University, a group of policymakers, technologists, and students gathered last week to tackle a question that will shape the nation's future: How can Lesotho embrace artificial intelligence without losing its soul?

The "Responsible AI for Lesotho" symposium, hosted by the Ministry of Communications, Science and Technology in partnership with the African AI Observatory, marked the first formal dialogue on AI governance in the mountain kingdom. As generative AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and local African AI platforms become increasingly accessible, Lesotho faces both unprecedented opportunities and significant risks.

"We cannot afford to be spectators in the AI revolution," said Dr. Mpho Letšela, the ministry's director of digital transformation, in her opening address. "But we also cannot afford to be reckless. The choices we make today about AI governance will determine whether this technology becomes a tool for liberation or a new form of dependency."

Lesotho's relationship with artificial intelligence is still in its infancy, but signs of change are emerging. The National University of Lesotho launched its first AI research lab in 2024, focusing on natural language processing for Sesotho and other indigenous languages. Meanwhile, a handful of startups are experimenting with AI-powered agricultural advisory tools for smallholder farmers, and the health ministry is piloting AI-assisted tuberculosis screening in rural clinics.

Yet the digital divide remains stark. According to the Lesotho Communications Authority, only 38% of Basotho have reliable internet access, and smartphone penetration hovers around 45%. For many, AI remains an abstract concept — something happening in Silicon Valley or Johannesburg, not in the highlands of Qacha's Nek or the streets of Maseru.

"There's a real risk that AI development will bypass the majority of Basotho," warned 'Maseabata Mofolo, a researcher at the Lesotho Institute for Public Policy. "If we don't deliberately design AI systems that work for our context — that understand Sesotho, that account for our agricultural realities, that respect our cultural values — then AI will become another tool of exclusion."

The symposium's central theme was governance. Unlike the European Union, which has enacted comprehensive AI legislation through its AI Act, or South Africa, which is developing a national AI strategy, Lesotho has no formal AI policy framework. This regulatory vacuum leaves the country vulnerable to several risks:

  • Data privacy concerns: Existing data protection legislation is weak and poorly enforced, leaving citizens vulnerable to algorithmic surveillance and data exploitation.
  • Algorithmic bias: AI systems trained primarily on Western or South African data may not perform accurately for Basotho users, potentially leading to discriminatory outcomes in hiring, lending, or healthcare.
  • Job displacement: As automation spreads, Lesotho's textile and manufacturing sectors — major employers — could face significant disruption without adequate retraining programs.
  • Digital colonialism: Without local AI capacity, Lesotho risks becoming a market for foreign AI products rather than a creator of its own solutions.
"We're at a fork in the road," said Thabo Molapo, a software engineer who returned to Lesotho after a decade in the United States to co-found Maseru's first AI startup. "One path leads to us being consumers of AI, paying foreign companies for services that don't fully meet our needs. The other path leads to us becoming creators — building AI that reflects who we are as a people. The difference between those paths is policy."

Despite the challenges, the symposium also highlighted promising opportunities for AI-driven innovation in Lesotho:

Agricultural transformation: With over 60% of Basotho relying on subsistence agriculture, AI-powered weather forecasting, pest detection, and crop management systems could dramatically improve food security. The Lesotho Meteorological Services is already exploring partnerships with AI researchers to improve localized weather predictions.

Healthcare access: AI diagnostic tools could help bridge Lesotho's severe doctor shortage, particularly in rural areas where the nearest clinic may be hours away. The Ministry of Health is considering AI-assisted screening for HIV, TB, and maternal health complications.

Education: AI-powered personalized learning platforms could help address overcrowded classrooms and teacher shortages, providing students with customized support in Sesotho and English.

Financial inclusion: AI-driven credit scoring could help extend banking services to the large unbanked population, using alternative data sources to assess creditworthiness.

The symposium concluded with a series of recommendations that participants hope will form the basis of Lesotho's first National AI Strategy, expected to be drafted by early 2027:

1. Establish an AI Governance Council: A multi-stakeholder body including government, academia, civil society, and the private sector to coordinate AI policy development and oversight.

2. Strengthen data protection laws: Update existing legislation to align with international standards and ensure robust enforcement mechanisms.

3. Invest in digital infrastructure: Accelerate broadband expansion, particularly in rural areas, to ensure equitable access to AI tools and services.

4. Fund AI research and education: Create scholarship programs for AI-related degrees, support local research institutions, and integrate AI literacy into school curricula.

5. Promote inclusive design: Require that government-funded AI projects undergo community consultation and accessibility testing to ensure they serve all Basotho.

6. Develop ethical guidelines: Create a Basotho AI ethics framework grounded in Sesotho values like botho (ubuntu) — the philosophy of collective humanity and mutual responsibility.

For many students who attended the symposium, the conversation felt urgent and personal.

"I'm studying computer science, and I've been wondering if I need to leave Lesotho to build a career in AI," said Nthatuoa Makhakhe, a third-year student at the National University of Lesotho. "This symposium showed me that there are people here who are thinking seriously about AI, who want to build something for Lesotho, not just import things from elsewhere. That gives me hope."

Others emphasized the importance of including ordinary Basotho in AI conversations.

"We can't have these discussions only in Maseru, only in English, only among elites," said 'Mantsi Mofolo, a community organizer from Berea. "We need to talk to farmers, to market vendors, to teachers in rural schools. What do they want AI to do for them? What are they afraid of? Those voices need to be in the room when decisions are made."

As Lesotho prepares to develop its AI strategy, the country faces a choice that will define its technological trajectory for decades. Will AI be a tool that deepens existing inequalities, or one that helps bridge them? Will Lesotho be a consumer of AI or a creator? Will Sesotho and other local languages be left behind in the AI revolution, or will they be actively developed as AI languages?

For Dr. Letšela, the answer is clear — but the path requires deliberate action.

"Small nations like Lesotho cannot afford to wait for AI to be designed elsewhere and then adapt to it," she told the symposium. "We must be active participants in shaping the AI future. That means investing in our people, in our infrastructure, in our values. It means building AI that speaks Sesotho, that understands our context, that serves our communities. It's a big task, but it's also a great opportunity — to show that responsible AI is possible, and that even the smallest countries can lead."

The symposium ended with a commitment to reconvene in six months, with working groups already forming to draft specific policy proposals. In the meantime, the Ministry of Communications, Science and Technology has opened a public consultation portal, inviting Basotho to share their views on AI governance and innovation.

Speaking in an interview with the Agency on Thursday, a Mosotho man advocating for the proper implementation of the cultural norms and practices of Basotho Mr. Lasaro Sello mentioned that Basotho should not take their culture for granted since there are many things attached to it.
